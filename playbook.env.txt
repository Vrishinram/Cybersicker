1. PURPOSE & SCOPE
================================================================================

This playbook governs the detection, containment, eradication, and recovery
procedures for cybersecurity incidents affecting Internet of Things (IoT)
infrastructure managed by Cybersicker SOC. It applies to all connected devices
including industrial sensors, smart controllers, edge gateways, IP cameras,
and embedded firmware endpoints.

Severity Levels:
  SEV-1 (CRITICAL) — Active data exfiltration, ransomware execution, full botnet C2
  SEV-2 (HIGH)     — Confirmed lateral movement, C2 beacon detected, mass anomalies
  SEV-3 (MEDIUM)   — Suspicious traffic patterns, single-device compromise indicators
  SEV-4 (LOW)      — Policy violations, misconfigurations, informational alerts

2. STANDARD OPERATING PROCEDURES (SOPs)

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
2.1 SOP-001: IoT Botnet Infection Response
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Trigger: Device exhibits C2 beaconing behavior, participates in coordinated
         scanning, or sends outbound traffic to known botnet infrastructure.
         Common families: Mirai, Mozi, BotenaGo, Enemybot.

Severity: SEV-1 (CRITICAL)

Phase 1 — Detection & Triage (0–15 min):
  1. Cybersicker autoencoder flags anomalous traffic volume on device subnet.
  2. Correlate with VirusTotal — check destination IPs against threat intel.
     If ≥5 vendors flag the IP as malicious → escalate immediately.
  3. Capture a 60-second full packet dump (PCAP) from the affected VLAN.
  4. Identify all devices communicating with the flagged C2 IP(s).
  5. Tag the incident as BOTNET-***REMOVED***YYYY-MM-DD***REMOVED***-***REMOVED***SEQ***REMOVED*** in the SOC ticket system.

Phase 2 — Containment (15–60 min):
  1. IMMEDIATE: Apply ACL to block outbound traffic from infected device(s)
     to C2 IPs at the perimeter firewall and local switch port.
  2. Isolate the affected IoT subnet (VLAN segmentation) — do NOT power off
     the device yet (preserve volatile memory for forensics).
  3. Deploy network sinkhole for identified C2 domains to capture additional
     infected devices attempting to beacon.
  4. Block lateral movement: disable inter-VLAN routing for the compromised
     segment, permit only management traffic from SOC jump box.
  5. If ≥10 devices are infected → declare SEV-1 and activate war room.

Phase 3 — Eradication (1–4 hours):
  1. Capture firmware dump and RAM image before remediation.
  2. Factory reset all confirmed infected devices.
  3. Re-flash firmware from verified vendor baseline (check SHA-256 hash).
  4. Rotate ALL credentials: device passwords, API keys, MQTT broker tokens,
     and any shared PSK or certificate material on the subnet.
  5. Patch known vulnerabilities (CVE cross-reference with NVD).

Phase 4 — Recovery & Lessons Learned (4–24 hours):
  1. Reconnect devices one at a time; monitor for 30 min each.
  2. Validate autoencoder baseline is recalibrated post-recovery.
  3. Update firewall blocklists with all observed IOCs (IPs, domains, hashes).
  4. Conduct post-incident review within 48 hours.
  5. Update this playbook with new detection signatures.

2.2 SOP-002: ICMP Flood / DDoS Mitigation
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Trigger: Cybersicker detects ICMP traffic volume exceeding 10,000 packets/sec
         or anomaly threshold >1,000 events on a single subnet within 60 sec.

Severity: SEV-2 (HIGH)

Phase 1 — Detection & Classification (0–5 min):
  1. Autoencoder flags volumetric anomaly on target subnet.
  2. Classify attack vector:
     - ICMP Echo Flood (Ping Flood): High-rate ICMP Type 8 packets.
     - Smurf Attack: Spoofed source IP with broadcast destination.
     - ICMP Fragmentation: Oversized ICMP packets causing reassembly DoS.
     - UDP/SYN Hybrid: Mixed protocol flood targeting IoT gateways.
  3. Identify source IPs — check if internal (compromised device) or external.
  4. Determine blast radius: which critical sensors/actuators are degraded.

Phase 2 — Mitigation (5–30 min):
  1. RATE-LIMIT FIRST: Apply ICMP rate limiting (≤100 pkt/sec per source)
     at the edge router. Do NOT fully block ICMP — IoT devices rely on
     ICMP for health checks and path MTU discovery.
  2. If attack persists after rate-limiting:
     a. Enable upstream DDoS scrubbing (if available via ISP/CDN).
     b. Apply source-based blackhole routing for confirmed attack sources.
     c. Activate GeoIP blocking for traffic from non-operational regions.
  3. If attack is internal (compromised IoT device as reflector):
     a. Isolate the device per SOP-001 Phase 2.
     b. Check for Mirai/Mozi indicators — pivot to botnet response.
  4. Maintain critical sensor availability throughout — prioritize OT traffic.

Phase 3 — Recovery (30 min–2 hours):
  1. Gradually lift rate limits while monitoring for resurgence.
  2. Validate all IoT endpoint connectivity and sensor data integrity.
  3. Export netflow data and PCAP for post-incident analysis.
  4. Submit source IPs to VirusTotal and threat intel sharing platforms.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
2.3 SOP-003: IoT Ransomware Containment
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Trigger: Encrypted file indicators on IoT management server, ransom note
         detected, firmware lock on edge devices, or Cybersicker flags
         anomalous write patterns to device storage.

Severity: SEV-1 (CRITICAL)

Phase 1 — Immediate Containment (0–10 min):
  1. DISCONNECT the affected segment from the network IMMEDIATELY.
     Ransomware propagation speed on flat IoT networks: <4 minutes.
  2. Do NOT power off encrypted devices — volatile memory contains
     potential encryption keys.
  3. Isolate all management interfaces (SSH, Telnet, MQTT, CoAP, HTTP).
  4. Snapshot any VM-based IoT gateways or edge compute nodes.
  5. Activate out-of-band communication for SOC coordination.

Phase 2 — Scope Assessment (10–60 min):
  1. Inventory all affected devices by MAC address and firmware version.
  2. Determine ransomware family (check encrypted file extensions, ransom
     note text, and cross-reference with ID Ransomware database).
  3. Check for lateral movement to IT network — inspect inter-zone firewall
     logs, AD authentication logs, SMB traffic patterns.
  4. Scan VirusTotal for ransomware payload hash.
  5. Assess data loss: are sensor logs, calibration data, or certificates
     encrypted? Can they be rebuilt from backup?

Phase 3 — Eradication (1–8 hours):
  1. DO NOT PAY THE RANSOM — document this as organizational policy.
  2. Check No More Ransom Project (nomoreransom.org) for decryptors.
  3. If no decryptor available:
     a. Wipe and re-image all affected IoT management servers.
     b. Factory reset and re-flash all compromised edge devices.
     c. Restore configurations from last known-good backup.
  4. All passwords, certificates, tokens, and API keys on the affected
     subnet MUST be considered compromised — rotate everything.
  5. Identify initial access vector (phishing, exposed management port,
     default credentials) and close it before reconnecting.

Phase 4 — Recovery (8–48 hours):
  1. Reconnect devices to an isolated staging VLAN first.
  2. Monitor for 4 hours before promoting to production.
  3. Validate sensor data accuracy against physical measurements.
  4. File law enforcement report (IC3/local CERT) within 24 hours.
  5. Notify affected stakeholders per incident communication plan.

3. GENERAL IoT ANOMALY RESPONSE RULES
================================================================================

Rule 1: General IoT Anomalies
If an IoT device or sensor network triggers an anomaly threshold above 1,000
events, the system must immediately isolate the affected subnet to prevent
lateral movement. Cybersicker autoencoder reconstruction error >0.5 MSE
constitutes an anomaly trigger. Escalate to SOC analyst within 5 minutes.

Rule 2: External Malicious IP Connections
If a device connects to an external IP address flagged by more than 5 security
vendors on VirusTotal, automatically block the IP at the perimeter firewall and
dispatch an alert to the SOC team for physical device inspection. Log the full
connection metadata: timestamp, source/dest IP:port, protocol, bytes transferred.

Rule 3: DDoS or ICMP Floods
In the event of an ICMP flood, rate-limit the traffic before initiating a full
lockdown to maintain critical sensor availability. Never hard-block ICMP on IoT
subnets — use rate-limiting as the first response. Full lockdown is Phase 2 only.

Rule 4: Unauthorized Firmware Modification
If a device firmware hash does not match the approved baseline in the asset
inventory, immediately isolate the device and treat as a potential supply-chain
compromise. Capture firmware image for reverse engineering before wiping.

Rule 5: Credential Brute-Force Detection
If Cybersicker detects >50 failed authentication attempts to any IoT device
within a 5-minute window, block the source IP, lock the target device account,
and trigger SOP-001 triage to check for botnet propagation.

Rule 6: Anomalous Data Exfiltration
If outbound data transfer from an IoT subnet exceeds 3x the 7-day rolling
average, flag as potential data exfiltration. Capture netflow, identify
destination, and cross-reference with VirusTotal threat intelligence.

4. MITRE ATT&CK MAPPING — TOP 5 IoT TACTICS
================================================================================

Tactic 1: Initial Access — Default Credentials (T0819/T1078)
  Attack: Adversaries use factory-default usernames and passwords to access
          IoT devices (admin/admin, root/root, etc.). Mirai variants scan
          for 60+ known default credential pairs.
  Detection:
    - Monitor authentication logs for successful logins from unexpected IPs.
    - Cybersicker flags devices with default credentials during asset scan.
    - Alert on any Telnet/SSH session from external addresses to IoT VLAN.
  Mitigation:
    - Enforce credential rotation policy: all defaults changed within 24hr
      of deployment.
    - Deploy certificate-based authentication where firmware supports it.
    - Network segmentation: IoT management ports NOT accessible from internet.

Tactic 2: Command and Control — C2 Beaconing (T1071/T1573)
  Attack: Compromised IoT devices establish encrypted or obfuscated channels
          to attacker infrastructure. Common protocols: HTTP/S, DNS tunneling,
          IRC, custom TCP on high ports.
  Detection:
    - Cybersicker autoencoder detects periodic outbound connection patterns
      with consistent intervals (beaconing jitter analysis).
    - DNS query analysis: flag domains with high entropy or DGA characteristics.
    - VirusTotal IP reputation check on all new external connections.
    - Monitor for connections to Tor exit nodes or known proxy services.
  Mitigation:
    - Enforce DNS filtering: IoT devices resolve only through internal DNS.
    - Whitelist outbound connections: IoT devices should only reach known
      vendor update servers and internal management systems.
    - Deploy TLS inspection on IoT gateway egress points.

Tactic 3: Lateral Movement — Exploitation of Remote Services (T0866/T1210)
  Attack: After initial foothold, adversary pivots across the flat IoT network
          exploiting unpatched services (UPnP, MQTT without auth, CoAP, SNMP).
  Detection:
    - Alert on any device-to-device connection not in the baseline communication
      matrix (Cybersicker builds this during learning phase).
    - Flag new internal port scans originating from IoT devices.
    - Detect ARP spoofing or abnormal ARP table changes on IoT switches.
  Mitigation:
    - Micro-segmentation: each device class gets its own VLAN.
    - Disable UPnP and unnecessary discovery protocols.
    - Enable 802.1X port-based access control on IoT switch ports.

Tactic 4: Impact — Denial of Service (T0814/T1498/T1499)
  Attack: Adversary overwhelms IoT devices or gateways with volumetric traffic
          (ICMP flood, SYN flood, amplification attacks) to disrupt operations.
  Detection:
    - Cybersicker autoencoder detects traffic volume spikes beyond 3 sigma.
    - Rate monitoring on ICMP, UDP, SYN packets per second per subnet.
    - Alert on bandwidth utilization >80% on IoT uplink interfaces.
  Mitigation:
    - Rate limiting at edge and access layer switches.
    - Deploy upstream DDoS protection for internet-facing IoT services.
    - Implement BCP38/BCP84 anti-spoofing at network boundary.
    - See SOP-002 for full response procedure.

Tactic 5: Collection & Exfiltration — Sensor Data Theft (T0802/T1041)
  Attack: Adversary harvests sensitive telemetry, environmental data, camera
          feeds, or proprietary sensor algorithms from compromised IoT devices.
  Detection:
    - Monitor outbound data volume per device against established baseline.
    - Flag any new external destinations receiving data from IoT subnets.
    - Detect unusual protocols or encrypted channels from devices that normally
      communicate in plaintext (e.g., MQTT suddenly wrapped in TLS to unknown IP).
  Mitigation:
    - Encrypt data at rest on IoT devices where supported.
    - Implement DLP rules on IoT gateway egress.
    - Network monitoring with full PCAP capability on critical subnets.

5. NIST COMPLIANCE — IoT SUBNET ISOLATION FRAMEWORK
================================================================================

Aligned with NIST Cybersecurity Framework (CSF) 2.0 and NIST SP 800-183
(Networks of Things).

IDENTIFY (ID):
  ID.AM-1: Maintain a real-time asset inventory of all IoT devices including
           MAC address, firmware version, communication baseline, and owner.
  ID.AM-2: Map all IoT network segments, data flows, and trust boundaries.
  ID.RA-1: Conduct quarterly risk assessments on IoT infrastructure.
  ID.RA-5: Threat intelligence feeds integrated into Cybersicker for
           continuous IOC matching.

PROTECT (PR):
  PR.AC-1: IoT devices on dedicated VLANs — never on corporate flat network.
  PR.AC-5: Principle of least privilege: IoT devices communicate only with
           their designated gateway and management server.
  PR.DS-2: All data in transit encrypted (TLS 1.2+ minimum).
  PR.IP-1: Firmware integrity validation using cryptographic signatures.
  PR.PT-3: Network segmentation enforced at Layer 2 (VLAN) and Layer 3 (ACL).

  Subnet Isolation Architecture:
    VLAN 100 — IoT Sensors (read-only telemetry devices)
    VLAN 110 — IoT Actuators (devices that execute commands)
    VLAN 120 — IoT Gateways (edge compute and protocol translators)
    VLAN 200 — SOC Management (Cybersicker, SIEM, jump boxes)
    VLAN 300 — Quarantine (isolated segment for compromised devices)

  Inter-VLAN Rules:
    - VLAN 100 → VLAN 120: ALLOW (sensor data to gateway only)
    - VLAN 110 → VLAN 120: ALLOW (command acknowledgments only)
    - VLAN 120 → VLAN 200: ALLOW (telemetry to SOC for analysis)
    - VLAN 200 → ALL:      ALLOW (management access)
    - VLAN 300 → ALL:      DENY  (quarantine is fully isolated)
    - ALL → VLAN 300:      DENY except VLAN 200 (SOC forensic access only)
    - DEFAULT:             DENY ALL (zero-trust baseline)

DETECT (DE):
  DE.AE-1: Cybersicker autoencoder establishes traffic baselines per device.
  DE.AE-2: Anomaly thresholds tuned per device class (cameras vs. sensors).
  DE.CM-1: Network monitoring on all inter-VLAN traffic.
  DE.CM-7: Continuous monitoring for unauthorized devices (rogue AP, unknown MAC).
  DE.DP-4: Detection information communicated to Cybersicker in real-time.

RESPOND (RS):
  RS.RP-1: This playbook is the primary incident response plan for IoT.
  RS.CO-2: Incidents reported to stakeholders within 1 hour of confirmation.
  RS.AN-1: Forensic analysis performed on quarantined devices (VLAN 300).
  RS.MI-1: Automated containment: Cybersicker triggers VLAN 300 quarantine
           when anomaly score exceeds threshold.
  RS.MI-2: Lateral movement blocked within 5 minutes of detection.

RECOVER (RC):
  RC.RP-1: Recovery follows the phased approach in SOPs above.
  RC.IM-1: Post-incident review within 48 hours; playbook updated accordingly.
  RC.CO-1: Public communication plan activated if customer data is affected.

6. ESCALATION MATRIX
================================================================================

  SEV-4 (LOW):
    Response Time: 4 hours
    Handler: SOC Analyst (L1)
    Actions: Log, monitor, update ticket

  SEV-3 (MEDIUM):
    Response Time: 1 hour
    Handler: SOC Analyst (L2)
    Actions: Investigate, contain if confirmed, notify team lead

  SEV-2 (HIGH):
    Response Time: 15 minutes
    Handler: SOC Lead + IR Team
    Actions: Execute relevant SOP, isolate affected subnet, begin forensics

  SEV-1 (CRITICAL):
    Response Time: IMMEDIATE
    Handler: CISO + Full IR Team + Legal
    Actions: Activate war room, execute SOP, preserve evidence, prepare
             stakeholder notification, engage law enforcement if required

7. EVIDENCE PRESERVATION & CHAIN OF CUSTODY
================================================================================

For all SEV-1 and SEV-2 incidents:
  1. Capture full PCAP of affected subnet (minimum 60-second window).
  2. Image volatile memory of compromised devices before power-off.
  3. Dump firmware from affected devices (binary image + hash).
  4. Export all Cybersicker autoencoder logs and anomaly scores.
  5. Screenshot VirusTotal results for all flagged IPs and hashes.
  6. Store all evidence in write-once storage with SHA-256 integrity hashes.
  7. Maintain chain of custody log: who accessed evidence, when, and why.
  8. Retain evidence for minimum 12 months or as required by regulation.
